Gopher & Chinchilla

Both trained at the same compute budget: C = 5.76×10²³ FLOPs. They sit far to the right of the experimental data, in the extrapolation regime of all scaling laws.

Gopher (280B) N = 280B · D = 300B tokens L̂ ≈ 1.993 (undertrained) Kaplan-era recipe: big model, few tokens
Chinchilla (70B) N = 70B · D = 1.4T tokens L̂ ≈ 1.937 (compute-optimal) 4× smaller, 4× more data than Gopher

The Approach 1 frontier predicts N = 69.3B at this compute — matching Chinchilla almost exactly. The Kaplan curve predicts N = 491B — 4× over-sized relative to compute-optimal.

Chinchilla — isoFLOP Scatter 114 pts · 9 slices

Extracted from Figure 4 (right) via PDF vector-graphics parsing. 9 groups each hold C = constant; D = C/6N adjusts. The scatter reveals the loss sweet spot for each compute budget.

The raw frontier picks the discrete minimum per slice — jagged due to shared model-size grids. The smooth frontier fits N∝C⁰·⁴⁷⁵ through those 9 minima.

Smooth dashed curves at the same 9 FLOPs budgets as the scatter. Compare directly to the real points to see where the fit over- or under-predicts. The three terms capture: irreducible text entropy (E), model capacity error (A/Nα), and data sub-optimality (B/Dβ).

All three agree that at Gopher's compute the optimal N ≈ 40–70B (vs Gopher's 280B).

Prior Scaling Laws

Kaplan et al. (2020) (N∝C⁰·⁷³) — Predicts N≈491B at Gopher budget. The steep exponent meant the field was building ever-larger undertrained models.

Henighan et al. (2020) (N∝C⁰·⁷³) — Validates Kaplan for language; different coefficient gives N≈154B at Gopher budget.
